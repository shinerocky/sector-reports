Sector Thesis

Three consecutive 20%+ growth years — the first time in semiconductor history — driven by an AI CapEx supercycle that shows no sign of stopping but carries embedded concentration risks that will define 2027.

The semiconductor industry is on track to hit $975B in 2026 — approaching $1 trillion for the first time. But this figure obscures a profound bifurcation: AI-facing segments (GPU, HBM, advanced packaging, foundry) are growing at 26–53% annually, while consumer-facing segments (PC, mobile) face -8% to -10% headwinds from memory price pass-throughs. The bull case is institutional — 75% of $660-690B hyperscaler CapEx is committed AI infrastructure, not speculation. The bear case is structural — $700B in hyperscaler CapEx funds only $200-250B in identifiable AI revenue, and the depreciation cliff hits 2027-2029.

CoWoS: The Hidden Bottleneck

  • Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate is required for HBM integration with GPU dies
  • TSMC CoWoS capacity is booked 18-24 months out — physically constrained
  • This, not wafer starts or NVIDIA chip design, is the binding constraint on AI GPU supply
  • TSMC expanding CoWoS aggressively: Fab C+, new sites in Taiwan and Arizona
  • Custom ASIC players (Broadcom) also require CoWoS — competition for capacity
Key Insight — CoWoS is the Real Constraint: Most investors focus on wafer starts and node yields. The actual binding constraint on AI accelerator supply in 2025-2026 is CoWoS advanced packaging capacity. Every H100, H200, B200, and MI300 series chip requires CoWoS to bond HBM stacks. TSMC has a near-monopoly on CoWoS at advanced packaging — the backlog is 18-24 months. TSMC's $54B CapEx is heavily weighted toward CoWoS expansion, which means the revenue upside from this investment flows entirely to TSM shareholders.

AI Supercycle or Bubble?

The most important question in semiconductors. The bull case is compelling but structurally dependent on AI revenue monetization that hasn't yet materialized at scale.

Bull Case — Supercycle

  • Institutional capital: 75% of $660-690B hyperscaler CapEx is AI-directed — this is board-approved, multi-year committed spend, not speculative bets
  • Real cash flows: NVIDIA $215.9B FY2026 at 75% margins = real earnings, not narrative; SK Hynix margins exceeding TSMC in Q4 2025
  • Physical supply constraints: HBM sold out, CoWoS booked 18-24 months — genuine scarcity, not manufactured demand
  • Historic growth: Three consecutive 20%+ growth years — unprecedented in semiconductor history
  • Sovereign AI demand: Government AI spending in Middle East, SE Asia, and Europe partially immune to US economic cycle; geopolitically motivated
  • Scale effects compounding: Every dollar of AI training creates models that enable more revenue-generating applications, creating a reflexive demand loop

Bear Case — Bubble Risk

  • Monetization gap: $700B hyperscaler CapEx vs ~$200-250B combined identifiable AI revenue — the gap must close by 2027-2028 or CapEx discipline snaps
  • DeepSeek efficiency risk: Algorithmic improvements (distillation, MoE) could dramatically reduce compute needed per AI task — demand destruction at the model layer
  • Depreciation cliff: $400B+ in 2025 CapEx depreciates over 5-7 years — replacement cycle is not guaranteed at same scale if AI ROI disappoints
  • Amazon FCF destruction: Amazon turning FCF-negative in 2026 due to AI investment; $1.5T projected hyperscaler debt issuance — credit event risk
  • ASIC structural erosion: Broadcom/Google/Amazon custom chips structurally erode NVIDIA's pricing power at inference — margin compression in 2027-2028
  • Concentration risk: <0.2% of semiconductor units (AI chips) drive ~50% of revenue growth — one demand shock creates system-wide reversion
Our View: The evidence strongly supports "supercycle" over "bubble" for 2026, but the 2027-2028 window is genuinely uncertain. The key differentiator: the 2000 dot-com bubble was speculation about future revenue; the current AI cycle is actual institutional procurement of physical infrastructure generating real margins. The risk is not that AI fails — it's that AI succeeds more efficiently than expected, reducing the hardware intensity per dollar of AI workload. DeepSeek-class algorithmic efficiency is the signal to watch.

Geopolitical Landscape

Trade policy is temporarily supportive, but the structural risks are building — particularly China's rare earth countermoves and the CHIPS Act timeline gap.

US-China Export Controls Easing Short-Term

  • Export controls "cooled down" per East Asia Forum (March 2026) — diplomatic de-escalation
  • Tariffs on Chinese chips: 0% currently, scheduled to escalate June 2027
  • NVIDIA China share: dropped from 90%+ to ~50% due to buy-local mandates
  • China domestic AI chip ecosystem (Huawei Ascend, Cambricon) scaling — 3-5 year threat
  • Window of relative stability through H2 2026 — take advantage for portfolio positioning

China Rare Earth Risk Underappreciated

  • China controls ~95% of global gallium supply — essential for GaN power devices
  • GaN used in: EV fast chargers, 5G base stations, defense electronics, data center power
  • Potential countermove against US semiconductor export controls: restrict gallium exports
  • 18-24 month lag before alternative supply chains (Australia, Japan) can scale
  • This risk is underpriced in analog/power names; monitor for Chinese export restriction announcements

CHIPS Act & US Reshoring Long-Term Play

  • TSMC Arizona Fab 21: N3 production beginning 2025-2026; N2 Arizona by 2028
  • Intel Ohio, Samsung Texas, Micron Idaho fabs receiving federal subsidies
  • Reality check: No alternative to TSMC for leading-edge chips at volume within 5 years
  • US reshoring cost premium: 40-50% higher manufacturing cost vs Taiwan equivalent
  • Long-term strategic importance; near-term competitive moat for TSMC remains intact
